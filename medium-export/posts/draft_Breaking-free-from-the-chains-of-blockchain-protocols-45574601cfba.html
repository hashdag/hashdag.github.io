Breaking free from the chains of blockchain protocols

We’ve been working on blockDAG protocols for several years now, and I figure it’s about time to make these ideas more accessible to the broader audience. I am therefore writing a series of blog-posts with the above title (taken from my presentation in Scaling Bitcoin Hong Kong). The series will cover the basics of BlockDAG Technology, survey of different blockDAG protocols, explanation of research questions and results, and finally some discussion on how blockDAG systems should be layered and the potential of this layered approach to address more than just the Scalability challenge. Concretely:

  • Inclusive blockchain protocols
  • The DAG mining protocol, the DAG framework
  • Incentives and selfish mining in DAG protocols
  • The SPECTRE protocol and followups
  • Self stabilization and network split resistance
  • Storage vs trust in a scalable system
  • Dag as a Service (DaaS)
  • miscellaneous 

The more technical posts will summarize what will hopefully become my PhD dissertation, under the supervision of my PhD advisor Aviv Zohar, to which I’m in debt for this entire research project (be sure to follow him on Medium at Aviv Zohar). If you take special interest in these posts, or you would like me to survey a specific topic, please let me know.

From blockchains to blockDAGs

The use of block DAGs instead of chains was first suggested in the Inclusive Blockchain Protocols paper from Financial Crypto ’15, coauthored by Yoad Lewenberg, Aviv Zohar, and myself. The assumption underlying this entire research is that we’ve scaled up the system to a certain level where propagation delays are non-negligible, orphan rate is high, and the blockchain no longer resembles a single neat chain. Indeed, increasing the throughput can be done either by creating blocks much more frequently (less than 10 seconds per block, say) or/and creating very large blocks, both of which increase the effect of network latency. If this is not yet the case, that is, if propagation delay is still negligible and the main chain does not suffer spontaneous forks, then this whole discussion about DAGs is largely irrelevant.

BlockDAG is not a better solution than blockchain. It is a better paradigm, and a more general one at that. Looking back, our initial insight was that instead of instructing each new block to choose a single chain to extend — the longest one, in the case of Bitcoin — we might as well ask the block to let us know of all chains that existed at the time of its creation, and we will already decide for it which chain it extends. Technically, the result is a block DAG, a Directed Acyclic Graph of blocks. Conceptually, it is a structure where every block references the tips of the DAG that existed prior to its creation (as observed locally by the miner that created it). These parallel tips might contain conflicting transactions, and so we must decide on some consensus rule.

…well, we could simply use Bitcoin’s rule — adopt the longest chain in the DAG and ignore all blocks off this chain (aka orphans). Our protocol would then coincide with Bitcoin. Alternatively, we can follow the GHOST protocol to select the DAG’s main chain.

In fact, it is easy to see that a DAG can simulate any chain-selection rule, as the DAG merely adds information about blocks and events, namely, which blocks were already received by the miner node when it created its new block. Of course, we are not guaranteed that each and every miner will provide this information fully, or that this information will be sufficient to achieve the desired performance of our system. But we are guaranteed that information that is provided is correct (because hash references cannot be manipulated or guessed ahead of time), and that honest miners will provide the information as instructed.

Incidentally, this line of reasoning resembles a neat result from Mechanism Design Theory, called “The Revelation Principle”.

The Revelation Principle

Say you want to carry out an auction. You must decide and commit to the auction’s rules in advance, how the winner(s) is decided, how much they pay, etc. Obviously, these decisions will influence the participants’ behaviour. Under two different mechanisms, the same bidder may choose to place entirely different bids, even though its true valuation of the sold item hasn’t changed. The Revelation Principle asserts, roughly speaking:

“The outcome of any given auction mechanism can be achieved by an incentive compatible mechanism where all bidders are better off revealing their true valuation of the item rather than placing artificially low or high bids.”

The proof is relevant, and goes along the lines of: We can mimic the dynamics of the original auction by asking each participant to reveal his real valuation of the item, compute for him his best bidding strategy (which he would have done by himself, presumably, in the original setup), and finally simulate the original auction mechanism by feeding it these strategic bids (rather than the participants’ real valuations).

The resemblance to blockDAGs may be a bit of a stretch, nonetheless the takeaway message remains, that no harm is done by letting each block reveal all available chains, and that we can still mimic and simulate the behaviour of any protocol suggested and formalized under the previous chain-based paradigm. (Moreover, we have now allowed ourselves more degrees of freedom in dictating the final outcome. We’ll get to this later).

Inclusive: utilizing off-chain blocks

Obviously, the benefit of the DAG setup is not merely its ability to simulate chain protocols; that wouldn’t be much of a help. Once a chain has been selected, we can incorporate the contents of off-chain blocks (aka orphans, but I prefer this term) into our UTxO. Consistency will be preserved by considering off-chain transactions as valid only as long as they do not contradict the main chain, up until the point where the main chain referenced them. More formally, we traverse the DAG in some topological order that additionally respects the rule that a main chain block precedes any off-chain blocks that it didn’t reference directly or indirectly; then, we accept each transactions that is consistent with those approved so far. We refer to this construction of the UTxO as the Inclusive rule or protocol. Inclusive is thus a generic protocol, as it assumes an underlying chain-selection rule.

See illustration in Figure 1.



The Inclusive protocol introduces a situation which is perhaps peculiar to Bitcoiners: a block that is valid, but which contains invalid transactions. Indeed, the aforementioned traversal of the DAG might result in a situation where some off-chain block is considered valid, is rewarded coins, but some of its transactions are thrown away because they conflict with or duplicate a transaction contained in a main chain block. The blockchain then does not represent the set of valid transactions, rather the order on the set of mined transactions.

Benefits of Inclusive

The first implication of Inclusive is added throughput, due to the inclusion of more transactions, namely, those contained in off-chain blocks. However, on second thought, this is not as simple as it sounds. If off-chain blocks merely contained the same set of transactions as the main chain blocks that were mined in parallel to them, no new transactions have been confirmed, and we gained nothing in throughput. Some mechanism is needed to make sure that off-chain blocks contain mostly unique content. Such a game-theoretic mechanism was the subject of our Inclusive paper, and I will dedicate a separate blog-post for this topic. (In short, we show that miners will be incentivized to randomize over their mempool when selecting new transactions for their next block, guaranteeing that only few transactions appear in blocks that were mined in parallel.)

Another benefit of Inclusive is the added Fairness. Off-chain blocks can be rewarded with minted coins just as main chain blocks are, and can earn the fees from valid and unique transactions they have contributed to the UTxO. The rewards from mining are then more fair, i.e., correspond better to the miner’s relative hashrate, as poorly communicating miners that don’t usually make it to the main chain are still rewarded. This too will also be discussed in the blog-post about Inclusive.

Chainless protocols

Note that combining Inclusive with, e.g., Bitcoin’s longest chain rule does not provide additional security to the chain: An attacker can ignore off-chain blocks, avoid referencing them, and suffer no consequences to its own chain. In particular, any attack scheme that is successful in Vanilla Bitcoin will succeed in Inclusive Bitcoin as well. For instance, Inclusive Bitcoin is as susceptible as vanilla Bitcoin to the double-spending attack whereby the attacker overrides the public chain using a longer chain that it mined secretly.* Since this attack (and others) becomes easier as the throughput is increased, we must artificially restrict it.

This vulnerability of chains motivated us to consider DAG protocols that do not rely on a chain altogether. Instead, we are seeking a protocol that outputs a full order over the DAG’s blocks, without discriminating between main chain and off-chain blocks. Defining precisely the desired properties of such a protocol is beyond the scope of this blog-post, but in general we want this order to become eventually agreed upon by all parties (and quickly so). Consistency would be preserved, again, by traversing the DAG in the prescribed order and accepting every transaction that is consistent with those approved so far.

Importantly, the benefits of more throughput and fairness remain intact when using a chainless protocol.

In terms of Information, the topology of the DAG provides us with information about the causal order of block-creation events: if block Y admits a path in the DAG to block X, then it was provably created after X. But this relation is partial, and many blocks are incomparable via the DAG topology. The ordering protocol should fill in the gap by extending this partial ordering into a full one, and achieve consensus on its final result.

This challenge of ordering the DAG lead to the development of SPECTRE, and other protocols. More on this journey in future posts.

In summary

BlockDAG is a paradigm where each block references several predecessors. It is of value only when the system is scaled up and many spontaneous forks to the public chain form. The DAG may contain conflicting transactions. The simple yet naive approach (which we took in our Inclusive work) would be to apply some chain-selection rule, and approve any off-chain transaction that is consistent with the main chain. Alternatively, we can utilize the DAG structure more intelligently by ordering its blocks without relying on an auxiliary chain. This is the SPECTRE approach. Either way, the incorporation of transactions from many blocks increases both the throughput and the fairness of the system, as more blocks become part of the game.

“Bitcoiners of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains!“


Notes:
* Even in GHOST, where off-chain blocks add weight to and secure the main chain, adding the Inclusive links from main-chain blocks to off-chain ones does not impact the security. Thus, conceptually, GHOST should be seen as using a tree structure, and Inclusive GHOST as using a DAG structure. Otherwise put, GHOST uses off-chain blocks’ headers to select the heaviest chain, whereas Inclusive GHOST would additionally utilize off-chain blocks’ contents.
